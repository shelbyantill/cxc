iNode Filesystem

In my Operating Systems class, I developed a custom filesystem supporting operations like open, create, read, write, and delete. I used C to implement an iNode structure with direct blocks and a single indirect block, managing up to 512 files and directories. The filesystem utilized bitmaps to track available space and read/write data on a simulated disk. This project demonstrated my understanding of file systems and low-level disk management.

Lexical Analyzer

In my Programming Languages course, I developed a lexical analyzer to simulate a step in the compilation process. The analyzer parsed a stream of characters from a simple programming language, using regular expressions to identify and group lexemes into tokens such as identifiers, constants, and symbols. These token objects were then returned to a mock parser. This project demonstrated my ability to implement fundamental concepts of compilers and regular expressions.

MLFQ Scheduler

In my Operating Systems class, I simulated a Multi-Level Feedback Queue scheduler in C, demonstrating my ability to model complex scheduling algorithms and manage CPU resources efficiently. The scheduler implemented four queue levels, each using round-robin scheduling with varying quantum sizes and demotion/promotion criteria. This design prioritized highly interactive processes, ensuring they received CPU time while allowing other processes to interact with I/O or remain idle.

Digital Media Game

In my Digital Media Programming course, I created an interactive game where players control a hot air balloon with a joystick, utilizing an Arduino. The goal of the game was to navigate a hot air balloon through a sky filled with obstacles and avoid collisions. A life would be lost with each collision. The game ramps up in difficulty as the number and speed of obstacles increase. An LED changes color to indicate the number of lives remaining. The objective is to survive the full 30 seconds.

In my public speaking course, I argue that the school system's reliance on grades and standardized tests as measures of success is both ineffective and detrimental to students. Drawing from my personal experience in a competitive magnet school, I discuss how the pressure to perform academically negatively affects students' self-esteem and mental health. I also highlight that grades fail to capture important qualities such as emotional intelligence, creativity, and leadership skills. Citing examples of successful individuals like Steve Jobs and Bill Gates, I emphasize that true success cannot be measured by academic performance alone. In conclusion, I encourage the audience to critically evaluate the grading system and recognize that success extends far beyond test scores.
